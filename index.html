Confidence Score Methodology

The confidence score of 88% is a composite metric computed from two independent factors:

1. Data Completeness (92%): Weighted average of coverage across all 6 sensor families. Weights: Accelerometer 25%, Strain 25%, Temperature 10%, Inclinometers 15%, Anemometer 10%, InSAR 15%. Accelerometers and strain gauges carry the highest weight because they directly measure structural response. Anemometers are weighted for environmental context. InSAR provides macro-scale validation (~12-day revisit, 2023–2025).

2. Cross-Sensor Agreement (85%): Ratio of sensor families with GREEN RAG status to total families assessed (5/6). When independent sensor families independently converge on the same health verdict, confidence increases. A disagreement (e.g., dynamic YELLOW while all others GREEN) does not lower confidence — it increases diagnostic specificity.

Composite: Average of the two scores (92% + 85%) / 2 = 88%. This method ensures that neither data volume alone nor agreement alone can inflate confidence — both evidence quality and evidence convergence must be strong.

LHS vs RHS Correlation — Structural Symmetry Check

Sensor Configuration: Accelerometers are installed symmetrically on both sides of each span — RHS (Right Hand Side, e.g. 2AS1_z) and LHS (Left Hand Side, e.g. 2AS2_z). At each location (DS1, DS2, DS3), one sensor sits on each side of the bridge deck, capturing vertical (Z-direction) vibrations at 200 Hz sampling rate.

What We Measure: Daily vibration data (~1 minute windows) is converted into FFT and PSD spectra. The dominant modal frequencies (Mode 1, 2, 3) are extracted from each sensor independently. By comparing the same mode between the LHS and RHS sensor at each span, we validate structural symmetry.

Interpretation:

Close Agreement (DI < 5%)
Healthy structural symmetry. Both sides respond identically to excitation — no asymmetric damage.
Moderate Divergence (5–10%)
Monitor closely. Could indicate early bearing wear, localized stiffness change, or sensor drift.
High Divergence (> 10%)
Investigate immediately. Potential cracking, cable force asymmetry, or bearing degradation on one side.

Tracking over time enables early detection of evolving fatigue and asymmetric damage — before it becomes visually apparent. This positions the LHS-RHS comparison as a continuous structural health consistency check and early-warning metric, not just raw vibration monitoring.

The strain distribution across sections DS1–DS6 shows a clear pattern: DS1–DS2 (mid-span region) experience the highest stress ranges (3–4 MPa) while DS3–DS6 (near supports/quarter-spans) show lower ranges (0.75–1.5 MPa). No evidence of neutral axis shift, which would manifest as unexpected tension/compression reversal at support locations.

3. Stress Redistribution

Temperature compensation reveals that DS4 shows the strongest thermal coupling (r=0.93, R²=0.86) with near-zero residual drift (<0.001 MPa/mo), indicating purely elastic thermal response. In contrast, DS1 shows weaker coupling (r=0.21) with higher residual drift (up to 0.058 MPa/mo). This pattern is consistent with early-age concrete creep in the mid-span region rather than structural distress — creep redistributes stress from concrete to embedded steel over the first years of service. The drift rates are well within expected bounds for a structure of this age (commissioned 2024).

4. Fatigue Life Estimation

All demand/capacity ratios are well below 1.0 (maximum 0.164 at DS1), meaning all stress ranges are below the constant-amplitude fatigue limit (CAFL) of 24 MPa. Under the Palmgren–Miner damage accumulation rule, the cumulative damage D = Σ(ni/Ni) is effectively zero for the current monitoring period. Even projecting the observed stress ranges over a 100-year design life with daily train loading (~36,500 cycles), the fatigue damage accumulation remains negligible (D < 0.001). All sections within fatigue limits — no damage accumulation.

Engineering Notes — Scope of This Assessment

This report is a decision-support tool based on automated sensor data analysis. It is not a structural safety certificate. Risk scores are statistical metrics, not load ratings. All findings should be interpreted by qualified structural engineers with knowledge of the bridge design, construction history, and intended loadings. Data Quality & Coverage
▼
  • All sensors maintained 100% uptime. No gaps > 1 hour.
  • Accelerometers sampled at 200 Hz; analysis uses pre-processed FFT/PSD (0.667 Hz resolution).
  • Strain/temperature at 15-min intervals; hourly resampling for correlation analysis.
  • InSAR: ~12-day revisit, different cadence from ground sensors. Used as macro context only.
Analysis Methodology
▼
  • FFT: Welch method, 512-sample segments, 50% overlap, Hann window. Frequency range 0.5-10 Hz for structural modes.
  • Strain-Temperature: Linear regression; non-linear effects not modelled. T_ref = median temperature in period.
  • Anomaly detection: z-score based on weekly statistics; threshold configurable.
  • Train detection: Energy proxy from FFT mean diff (not direct train timetable).
  • Risk scoring: Weighted linear combination; weights user-adjustable.
Interpretation Caveats

Data Quality Assessment
Overall data completeness: 88%. The primary limitation is the anemometer sampling strategy (~2% of files), which captures daily patterns but may miss sub-hourly transient events. InSAR operates on a separate ~12-day revisit cadence and provides macro-scale context only. All other sensor families have >95% coverage with 15-minute or better resolution.
